The Children We Lose



Patricia Martínez Sastre
August 9, 2022



From the moment he was born, the odds were against Quince Rowe.

When doctors tested his blood, they found marijuana and the newborn was immediately entered into the foster care system. His parents had a long history of legal problems, involving drugs and domestic violence, and each had previous encounters with Arizona’s Department of Child Safety (DCS) over the welfare of earlier children.

The agency determined living with his parents was not safe for Quince, so they petitioned to have the boy legally separated from them. But a Maricopa County judge was unconvinced such a dramatic step was necessary, and in December of 2019, granted sole custody to the boy’s father, Terrance.

Less than a year later, he was dead from a fentanyl overdose, and Terrance Rowe was arrested.

"He had just started walking", recalled the child's grandmother Apryl Ramey in a phone interview. "He was a happy baby. Oh, yes, he was really happy.” Ramey believes the system failed the boy, not just in the months after he was born, but also once he was reunited with his father.

After Quince died, the agency produced a fatality report showing each prior allegation, but neither their attempt to intervene nor the services provided were enough to save the baby's life, a common outline among child maltreatment fatalities in America.

Since 2010, Arizona social workers had prior involvement with families —or victims— in more than half of the nearly 900 confirmed child abuse or neglect deaths in the state. That percentage reached a decade-time high of 69% in 2020, according to the state's most recent Child Fatality Review (CFR) report.

These reports are conducted by multidisciplinary teams in charge of reviewing all child’s deaths by state, their circumstances and risk factors involved in order to understand why they happened and prevent future ones. Only a small fraction of them are classified as child maltreatment fatalities.

While states use different methodologies to compile numbers and some do a more comprehensive job than others, the pattern is roughly the same: hundreds of children are killed each year despite the intervention of government child-care specialists.

In Texas, which runs the largest child welfare system in the country, 199 children died from maltreatment last year, the state's annual report shows, and 58% of them —or their caregivers— shared a prior history of involvement with the Department of Family and Protective Services (DFPS). Since 2010, on average, that has been the case in roughly half of all confirmed 2,460 child fatalities in the state.

In Colorado that percentage ranges from 36% of the total confirmed deaths in 2010 to 75% in 2020, according to the agency's child maltreatment annual reports. That year, the state confirmed the death of a total of 21 children whose families —or themselves— had 3-year prior or current history with child-protection workers.

Public officials and field workers say the most difficult part of their job is knowing how much a family is really struggling, or the degree of danger faced by their children, just from the evidence of what they see and learn after knocking on doors. Scholars and researchers believe part of the problem is that case workers often go into cases without enough knowledge of the history, length and severity of their prior involvement with the agencies.

Prior allegations of maltreatment, even unsustained ones, are the strongest predictor for injury mortality among toddlers under five, according to a 2011 California-based study conducted by Emily Putnam-Hornstein, a lead designer of predictive analytics at the University of North Carolina.

This means that children reported to social services are nearly six times more likely to die from intentional injuries —and twice the rate in deadly accidents— than demographically similar, unknown children; and public protection agencies know that.

"[Child neglect fatality] is a very difficult thing to predict that is going to happen, and you don't want to be overstepping and traumatizing a family when the likelihood of that event is actually very low", said Katherine Guffey, DCS's chief quality improvement officer, when asked about the increased prior CPS involvement among fatality cases.

While child abuse-related deaths are in fact a small percentage amid the 2.1 million screened-in referrals that reached the system in 2020, according to the last national Child Maltreatment report, experts consider each death preventable and belive that only structural changes —within the agencies, but also in the way services are delivered, technology used and families treated—will help protect the nation's most vulnerable.

TIED HANDS

Most states first developed child protection agencies in the 1960s after the publication of 'The Battered-Child Syndrome' by the pediatrician Henry Kempe, a shocking expose that made child maltreatment a matter of national public interest. The new agencies were charged with fielding complaints about children in danger, investigating referrals to their hotlines, and either offering assistance to troubled parents or, ultimately, initiating legal proceedings to remove the children.

The legal framework for this system is tied to the 1974 Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act (CAPTA), amended most recently in 2019, which made federal money available to help the states' efforts to prevent child abuse, investigate reports, and prosecute wrong-doers. It also established mandatory reporting laws when child abuse is suspected.

The system has traditionally prioritized out-of-home care. Local and state agencies devote nearly half of their total spending — a sum of $33 billion— to finance the costs of foster care, followed by the area of adoption and legal guardianship with 19%, according to a survey published last year by the non-profit Child Trends with 2018 financial data from 41 states.

Only around 15% is used on prevention efforts that, experts say, could help troubled families before a point of crises. And within prevention, concrete economic support such as housing or transportation, as well as substance abuse treatment and mental health services, are among the least funded in comparison to less effective, high-cost interventions, like parenting classes.

Congress passed in 2018 the Family First Prevention Services Act (FFPSA) as an effort to shift the emphasis towards prevention by providing federal funds for mental-health care and substance-use treatment in lieu of foster care placements, but critics fear little has changed on the ground.

"Most of the time no action will be taken at all unless the agencies assess that the child was in immediate danger", explained the US child welfare analyst Dee Wilson about the axiom that leads these public institutions, with scarce power of intervention until the child's life is considered endangered.

"But in reality these kids are living in a kind of a daily condition where their needs are not being adequately cared for, which doesn't necessarily mean they are always unsafe", he said, summing up one of the system's greatest dichotomy, how to effectively tackle chronic neglect caused by parents commonly suffering from mental health issues, drug abuse or domestic violence.

Child-protection agencies are only required to provide services, and establish a safety plan once children are considered unsafe, which means that they are likely to be severely harmed in the foreseeable future by some danger from their caregiver. If, on the contrary, the risk factors observed don't translate into an imminent danger, most likely nothing will be done.

ACCIDENTAL INTOXICATION

The night of Quince's death, Phoenix Police found two fentanyl pills on the couch —where Rowe and the baby had fallen asleep, according to a police report—, one more under the couch and several others in a drawer. The autopsy found Quince died of accidental fentanyl intoxication, and also found traces of Narcan, the drug commonly used to reverse an overdose, in his blood.

Quince's mother, Qnique Thues, had previously tested positive for marijuana and cocaine in a prior pregnancy in 2016, public records show, and had also had her parental rights terminated in relationship to an earlier son.

Arizona's DCS had accused her three times of exposing Quince and other children to illicit substance abuse and once to domestic violence, according to the agency's reports.

Terrance Rowe, meanwhile, had racked up four felony convictions involving weapons, burglary and drugs, according to court documents. When he was given Quince's custody in 2019, he already had a long history of involvement with the CPS through different mothers and children, the investigation file shows, and a pattern of neglect and substance use involving oxycodone and cannabis.

The state did try to find Quince a safer place than their parents tutelage, but the court rejected the intervention and placed the 4 month-old baby under his dad's supervision despite his addictions, restraining orders from an ex partner and the fact he was an absent figure for his older children.

During Rowe's trial, Maricopa Superior Court determined that he was "intellectually able to recognize threats, but failed to do so" in the case of Quince, and said that he failed to control his impulses with using marijuana.

Phoenix Police concluded, based on messages from Rowe's personal phone, that he was in the process of purchasing large quantities of fentanyl when the overdose happened, an allegation he denied in court. At the time, he had been unemployed for more than one year and was coping with financial stress.

FIVE DEATHS A DAY

Every year in the United States around 1,700 children die due to abuse or neglect, most before the age of three, while more than 600,000 are seriously harmed, according to data from the National Data Archive on Child Abuse and Neglect (NDACAN); an imcomplete voluntary reporting system established by the Children’s Bureau in 1988.

Newborns and toddlers —many times too young to crawl— perish by suffocation while co-sleeping with their parents or drown due to lack of adequate supervision. More rarely, data from the FBI Supplementary Homicide Report reveals, they suffer episodes of blunt force trauma enough to fracture their fragile bodies.

While it’s difficult to compare statistics across countries, the data most frequently cited shows the United States leads the developed world in child-abuse deaths, with a child-maltreatment death rate that has remained constant of about 2.5 per 100,000 children, more than triple Canada's or Australia's and eleven times that of some European countries such as Italy.

More than five children die every single day in America, yet there is consensus that number is a substantial undercount. States classify maltreatment differently —only some of them include deaths where maltreatment was a contributing factor, for instance— while fatal cases of medical neglect or sudden infant deaths (SIDS), experts say, can go unnoticed even after a forensic exam has been performed.

Also, in most states, police do not generally report abuse or neglect deaths to child welfare agencies, who then submit an incomplete picture to the Children's Bureau reporting system, while deaths occurring on tribal land or reservations aren't always included in the state review process.

"The actual number of child maltreatment fatalities is more than double the NCANDS total, but at least 3,000 children per year", said child welfare expert and former foster care worker Marie Cohen. This is in line with the estimate made by the national Commission to Eliminate Child Abuse and Neglect Fatalities (CECANF).

Established in 2014 by the Obama's Administration, under the Protect Our Kids Act, CECANF followed the mandate to shed some light on how to effectively mitigate the unfailing prevalence of child abuse and neglect fatalities in the country.

After two years of public hearings and meetings with government leaders, frontline actors and researchers —among others—, a total of twelve commissioners agreed in 2016 on the urgency to reform a "mainly reactionary" system unfit to safeguard millions of children at daily risk of harm.

"Our current approach waits until a child is severely injured before intervening with vital supports", said in the final report the chairman of the Commission, David Sanders, also executive vice-president at Casey Family Programs.

"It relies primarily on a single government agency to intervene with families who face complex and intersecting challenges. Too often, this approach inflicts significant additional trauma on the very children it seeks to protect", he concluded.

The Commission made a total of 114 recommendations. Among the most significant were a higher CPS screening rate, closer monitoring of infants and toddlers —the age groups most at risk—, access to real-time data about families, higher coordination with health care providers and more evidence-based programs like home visiting for families with newborns.



"Every single time a child dies you see the exact same failures", said Putnam-Hornstein, co-director of the Children's Data Network and a major proponent of predictive analytics reforms.


Some of its achievements, according to the Commission's executive director, are that its recommendations are currently being tested in five locations —including Indiana and Michigan child welfare systems— and that it has helped change the public perception that these fatalities are unpreventable and that there’s much value in investing to support families.

"We know that if we shift the system to make sure that families have what they need earlier on, then we would ideally see less children coming into foster care and also a less expensive intervention", said the Commission's head Amy Jantz Templeman, in line with studies that have shown that for each dollar spent on reducing childhood poverty, the country would save over seven dollars.

"That's really the direction I think the field is heading in", Templeman added.

But advocates regret that most deficiencies in the system they encountered back in 2014 are still present, such as lack of effective coordination between agencies or decades-old technology unable to speedily cross-reference old siblings' history with a new alleged victim, or to match birth certificates with parents who have already lost their parental rights or killed another child.

"Every single time a child dies you see the exact same failures", said Putnam-Hornstein, also co-director of the Children's Data Network and a major proponent of predictive analytics reforms. "You see a failure to share information across agencies, you see risk and safety tools that were filled out late or inaccurately and you see kind of poor supervision and sloppy casework".

SAME OLD BARRIERS

In many states, common underlying operational challenges like high caseloads, low wages among social workers, an elevated turnover, and staffing shortages especially after the Covid-19 pandemic, are also responsible for holding back real reforms within public agencies.

Overwhelmed by new cases, less recent ones simply become inactive; crucial family home visitings only take place once and key behavioral cues and risk factors —that could help protect children in the long run, according to experts— end up buried in cases transferred between withdrawal case managers.

Following the Covid-19 epidemic, services providers in Indiana reported experiencing "an unprecedented lack of workforce", more rampant for substance abuse treatment, and said to be facing crisis levels across the state, according to an interview with IARCA, an umbrella non-profit formed roughly by 90 community organizations.

A similar situation is taking place in Arizona's DCS, where 271 staff positions remained unfilled as of last May in comparison to just 59 two years ago, according to its latest monthly operational report. The agency is also facing a growing annual turnover among child welfare specialists of 57%.

Moreover, similarly to last year when the number of open cases increased 141% compared to those of 2020, the agency maintains today more than ​​11,500 open reports; and both cases under investigation and inactive have skyrocketed to over 2,400.

"The first couple of days you're probably going to have the most contact with that family because you're in the investigation gathering stage", said a former special investigator at Texas CPS, who acknowledged a shortage of staff and "overworked" caseworkers with up to 40 daily cases.

"But more than likely, after you get there, you talk to mom and dad, you talk to everybody in the house, you talk to that kiddo, the amount of times you're really gonna deal with that family is gonna be minimal", said who worked in the system from late 2019 to mid-2021.

According to Quince's grandmother, not a single case worker interviewed her. "We didn't exist till the end because none of us talked to nobody", Ramey added, an allegation DCS' public officials didn't comment on, arguing confidentiality issues.

She also said that, just a month before her grandson's death, Rowe was furious because Qnique had left him and threw an incendiary cocktail at her house, burning it partially. He also texted her daughter threatening "to kill everything she loves". Ramey said to have warned the police, but that officers decided to not intervene.

Phoenix Police confirmed the incident described and that no arrests were made. "If they had arrested him that day the situation wouldn't have escalated that far, I don't believe, as to kill my grandson", Ramey said heavily.

URGE TO CHANGE

Most voices in the research and advocacy communities warn that little will change until at least three pillars come together: the collection of better nationwide child neglect and abuse data, the use of statistical models to identify high-risk cases with acute needs, and on the front end, earlier and non-stigmatized access to adequate services within usually impoverished communities.

Supporters say predictive analytics, a new generation of algorithms able to crunch numerous data points about troubled families, would help social workers —courts, health providers and law enforcement— flag children most at risk of fatality and agencies better deploy what are often limited resources.

"The models very clearly show that we are flooding our system with a lot of calls while there's a very small slice of referrals and reports where not only death rates are much, much higher, but they also tend to involve families with a significant amount of history and very young children", said Putnam-Hornstein, who currently leads a pilot program in Los Angeles County.

Yet, others feel unsettled by relying so much on algorithms. They recognize their potential guidance power, but fear that these models will always be trained with racially biased data, and therefore, discriminate and facilitate interventions in Black and Latino communities, for decades already disproportionately targeted by these institutions.

To some reformers, also the fact that many families struggle shortly after being helped, or say to not have received adequate care at all, is a sign that even if potentially high risk cases like Quince's were prioritized through predictive analytics, the current system would continue to fail them. That's why they think community services need to be available sooner in the process as well as for families with infants reported to the hotline, but not investigated by CPS.



"That requires changing an organization. So it's a big, big shift that has to happen"-, said Rosalyn Bertram, professor at the University of Missouri-Kansas City".


Over the years Quince's parents were offered information or participated in community services, like counseling or substance abuse treatment, mainly as a condition to regain custody of older children temporarily under CPS supervision. Nevertheless, they continued to relapse and new allegations of neglect made their way to the hotline.

Along with setting prevention efforts one step earlier, some think that evidence-based services, this is practices scientifically proved to be effective such as home visiting programs, aren't sufficiently spread out neither count with the state infrastructural supports —qualified workforce, data systems, research centers— needed to maintain them in the long term.

"That's a major shift from the bureaucratic approach that most state systems are set up to do", said Rosalyn Bertram, professor at the University of Missouri-Kansas City and director of the journal Child & Family Evidence-Based Practice Consortium. "That requires changing an organization. So it's a big, big shift that has to happen".

Experts speak of a significant "science to service gap", in the words of Suzanne Kerns, director of transformative research at the Kempe Center, between what research has proved to be effective for families and what is actually available in community and primary care settings to tackle recurrent risk factors.

Public officials agreed that many parents currently have difficulties participating in services. They mentioned an apparent lack of motivation as one of the causes, but also transportation issues and paradoxically, mental health conditions or substance addictions that stop them from receiving the same help that would get them back on their feet.

Other parents simply distrust the whole system in the first place. CPS carry a long history of discrimination and those under investigation are socially stigmatized as neglectful caregivers, which ultimately put them at odds with child protection goals. Until that antagonistic view is not mitigated, experts say, families will be wary and generally more willing to cooperate with outside community organizations like Family Resource Centers.

After accepting a plea agreement, Rowe was sentenced in July 2021 to a total of six months in prison for "reckless and careless behavior that caused the death of a child" and three years on probation with domestic violence conditions and parenting classes, according to the final sentencing document.

The court determined that his substance abuse and parenting issues could actually be better "assessed and treated within the community", an approach that had failed for years, and described his deadly negligence as a "potential learning experience for the defendant". Rowe's lawyer, Brian Foutz, said he is currently unable to contact his former client for comment.

"Your Honor, I haven't been locked up since 2001. I realize I've made a few very bad mistakes but please allow me to make up for my actions and bad choices", Rowe wrote by hand last August in a court request to modify his jail conviction. He was released on the 15th of December.

Since Quince died, dozens of other Arizona infants have met a similar fate, and the statistics are sometimes numbing. But Quince's grandmother still suffers from the loss."I miss him. Everything. His smell, his mouth", said Ramey. "He put a choker on my neck and I've been wearing that choker on my neck ever since. I haven't taken it off ".
